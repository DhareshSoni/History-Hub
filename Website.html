THE INDIAN FREEDOM STRUGGLE

  • Before the widespread freedom movements, various small rebellions and resistance efforts challenged British expansion in India.
  • Battle of Plassey (1757): Marked the beginning of British dominance in India after defeating the Nawab of Bengal.
  • Sanyasi and Fakir Rebellions: Religious-led uprisings in Bengal and Bihar.
  • Paika Rebellion (1817): Armed rebellion in Odisha against British economic policies.
  • Vellore Mutiny (1806): One of the earliest military uprisings by Indian sepoys.

The First War of Independence (1857)

  • Also called the Sepoy Mutiny or 1857 Revolt, it was the first large-scale armed rebellion against British rule.
  • Sparked by grievances of Indian soldiers (sepoys) over cartridge greasing rumors.
  • Spread quickly across northern and central India.
  • Though ultimately suppressed, it shook British confidence and led to the dissolution of the East India Company.
  • The British Crown took direct control of India afterward (1858), beginning the British Raj era.

Formation of Indian National Congress (1885)

  • Founded as a forum for airing grievances and reform demands.
  • Initially moderate in approach, seeking dialogue with the British government.
  • Became the platform for political leadership of the freedom struggle.

Early Nationalist Movements (1885–1915)

  • Leaders like Dadabhai Naoroji, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Bipin Chandra Pal, and Lala Lajpat Rai emerged.
  • Called for self-rule (Swaraj), social reforms, and economic upliftment.
  • Partition of Bengal (1905): British policy to divide Bengal sparked widespread protest and Swadeshi movement.

Mahatma Gandhi and Mass Movements (1915–1947)

  • Mahatma Gandhi arrived in India in 1915 and transformed the freedom struggle with his philosophy of non-violence (Ahimsa) and civil disobedience.

Key Movements Led by Gandhi:

  • Champaran Satyagraha (1917): First successful use of non-violent protest to support indigo farmers.
  • Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922): Nationwide boycott of British institutions, schools, courts, and goods.
  • Salt March (1930): Protest against the British salt tax; marked by Gandhi’s 240-mile march to the sea.
  • Quit India Movement (1942): Mass protest demanding an immediate British withdrawal; many leaders imprisoned.

Other Important Movements and Contributions

  • Revolutionary Activities: Bhagat Singh, Chandrashekhar Azad, and others took a militant stance for independence.
  • Role of Women: Figures like Sarojini Naidu, Kasturba Gandhi, and Annie Besant played crucial roles.
  • Social Reform Movements: Efforts to end caste discrimination, promote education, and unify Indians.
